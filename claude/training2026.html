Training Overview

Your weekly schedule, key principles, and how the 11 weeks map onto your life constraints.

How to Execute This Plan on Real Roads

  • Lap buttonHit lap at the bottom of a climb, ride to effort, hit lap at the top. Review the lap average afterward. No Garmin workout mode needed — terrain is your interval timer.
  • One numberSet a screen showing 3-second power + HR. Keep one target in your head per ride ("stay under 150W today" or "this climb: 168–178W"). Glance at it, don't stare.
  • Commutes47% of commute time will be in Z1 regardless — stop-and-go kills structured intervals. Use commutes for easy spinning; push hills naturally when terrain allows. Don't try to time efforts around traffic lights.
  • Steep pitchesWhen grade makes Z2 impossible, switch to HR: above 148 bpm, soft-pedal until it drops. Let watts spike — the stage average is what matters, not every switchback.
  • Local climb timesOld La Honda ~22–28 min · Kings Mtn ~15 min · Tunitas lower ~18 min · Page Mill ~25 min. The climb sets the duration — ride it, don't fight the road to match a prescribed time.
  • Recovery between effortsAfter a threshold or SS climb lap, spin easy for 8–10 min minimum before the next effort. 1–5 min is not enough — HR won't recover and the next effort will be compromised. Quality over quantity.
  • Missing targetsLog it, note how it felt, move on. Consistency over weeks beats nailing every session.

Key Principles

  • Power first, HR second. Use power to set effort, HR to validate. If HR drifts high on Z2 days, back off the watts.
  • Keep Z2 truly easy. Most commutes and long rides should be Zone 2 (112–150W). Conversational pace. This builds the aerobic engine that carries you over 20 alpine passes.
  • The WW LDT rides ARE your training. Don't skip them. All LDT rides are tandem with Boen. The tandem naturally puts you in the C+ group (one step slower than D, ~12–13 mph average on hilly terrain). That is a solid steady aerobic load and exactly right for multi-day tour prep — the Alps will feel familiar.
  • Recovery is training. Commutes on Mon/Thu should be genuinely easy — Z1/Z2 only.

Equipment & Measurement

  • Power MeterPrimary effort metric. Lap average power is your guide on every interval.
  • HR MonitorSecondary check. High HR for the target power = fatigue/heat/illness. Rest.
  • FTP TestDo a 20-min test (take 95%) on Wed Apr 2 and again Wed May 13 to track progress.
  • Alps GearingConsider a compact 34T inner ring + 32T cassette. Col de l'Iseran at 52 km/hr descent needs aero too.
  • NutritionPractice eating 60–80g carbs/hr on all rides over 90 min. This is non-negotiable in the Alps.

Tandem Tips — Riding with Boen (Age 11)

Boen is an experienced tandem stoker who is now old enough and fit enough to contribute meaningfully. At 11, he should be generating 30–60W of real power on climbs — a genuine asset, not just extra weight. The dynamic shifts significantly from when he was younger.

Boen's Real Contribution at Age 11

  • Stoker PowerAt 11 and fit, Boen can realistically output 30–60W sustained on long climbs and 60–90W+ on flatter terrain. That's a meaningful percentage of the system's total demand.
  • System WeightTandem + Boen (~80–90 lbs) + you + loaded touring bags = ~370–420 lbs. Still heavy, but Boen's power partially offsets his weight — especially at Z2 pacing where his contribution is proportionally larger.
  • UpshotA 50W stoker contribution at 300W total system demand = 17% assist. This meaningfully changes your climbing speed vs. a passive stoker. The tandem should be faster than expected on moderate gradients.
  • Realistic Climb SpeedExpect 6–9 km/hr on moderate alpine climbs (6–8%). Slower than a solo rider but respectable for a loaded tandem with two people working.

Power Targets on the Alps (Tandem Captain)

  • Long Climbs (Boen active)125–148W captain output. With Boen contributing 40–60W, total system power is 165–208W — very workable for sustained Alpine climbs.
  • Sustained Multi-Col Days115–135W captain. Protect your legs for Day 3, 4, 5… Let Boen pace you down, not up.
  • Steep Ramps (>10%)143–162W captain for short surges. Boen's contribution drops on very steep grades — you carry more.
  • Monitor Boen's fatigueIf you feel the bike suddenly getting "heavier" on a climb, Boen has backed off. Check in verbally. Don't shame — just ask "you good?" and adjust your power up to compensate.

Communication — The Active Stoker Protocol

  • "Push!" / "Easy" — Signal when you need more or less from Boen. On a brutal ramp, "push!" rallies him. Cresting a summit, "easy" lets him recover.
  • "Cadence up" — If Boen is grinding too slowly on a steep pitch, his low cadence transmits uneven force. Prompt him to spin faster even if it means lighter pressure.
  • "Standing now" — Still essential. Both of you standing at the same time on a steep tandem ramp can be powerful, but it needs to be coordinated. Practice this before the tour.
  • "Tunnel!" / "Bump!" / "Braking!" — Non-negotiable. Boen still can't see forward. These calls let him brace, protect his hands, and stay engaged with the road.
  • Give him a job on descents. At 11, Boen can be bored on long technical descents. Have him call out road surfaces, check for cars, or count switchbacks. Engagement = better riding.

Training Boen Before the Tour

  • Get Boen on the tandem at least 3–4 times before Geneva. Real climbing rides, not just flat spins. He needs to build tandem-specific leg strength and cadence habits on grade.
  • An excellent pre-tour tandem ride: Old La Honda (3.5 mi, 1,300ft). If Boen can climb OLH on the tandem without fully gassing out, he's ready for the Alps.
  • Work on cadence consistency — a good stoker maintains 75–85 rpm and doesn't surge or coast erratically. This matters more than raw power.
  • Practice the standing start protocol together. Pick a consistent word, count to 3, stand simultaneously. Gets it second nature before the steep Alpine ramps.

Fueling Boen on Stage Days

  • At 11 and actively pedaling, Boen needs real fuel — not just snacks. 30–45g carbs/hr minimum on rides over 90 minutes. He will hit a wall without it, and an under-fueled stoker is effectively dead weight.
  • Give Boen his own stoker feed bag with gels, chews, or bars he actually likes. Make it his responsibility to eat on schedule — treating him like a real athlete increases engagement.
  • Hydration: kids underconsume. At café stops, Boen drinks before he's thirsty. This is non-negotiable in warm weather on 5+ hour days.
  • Café stops are essential morale management. European cycling culture is perfect for this — every Alpine village has coffee, cake, and a good view. Budget 20–30 min at mid-ride stops.

Tandem-Specific Gear Checklist

  • Gearing34T chainring + 32–34T cassette minimum. Even with Boen contributing, the loaded tandem on a 9% Alpine wall needs the granny gear.
  • BrakesTandem disc brakes get very hot on long Alpine descents. Carry spare brake pads. Consider a tandem drum brake if not already equipped.
  • Stoker BarA T-bar with multiple hand positions lets Boen shift his weight and posture on long climbs. At 11, he'll find a comfortable position if given the option.
  • Stoker Feed BagMounted on stoker handlebars. Makes Boen self-sufficient for nutrition — no interrupting captain focus mid-climb to hand back snacks.
  • Stoker MirrorA small mirror for Boen gives him forward visibility on descents, reduces startle responses, and keeps him mentally engaged with the road.
  • Train LogisticsFrom your 2025 Dolomites experience: tandems count as one bike in Germany, two in Switzerland. Factor this into your Geneva–Alps routing and any train bookings.

  • Sets × Reps: 2×12 @ 60–65% 1RM
  • Focus: Maintain strength without accumulating fatigue
  • Rest: 60–90 sec
  • Both sessions: Squat, RDL, Step-Up, Single-Leg Press, Core. No heroics.

Phase 4: Activation (May 26 – Jun 4)

  • No loaded lifting in the final 10 days before the tour.
  • Bodyweight only: Air squats 2×20, Glute bridges 2×20, Single-leg balance work, Hip 90-90 stretching
  • Core activation: Dead bug, bird-dog, Pallof press (light band)
  • Duration: 15–20 min max. This is mobility and activation, not training.
